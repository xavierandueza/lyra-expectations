02

Don't be an NPC.

NPC  →  PLAYER ONE

Lyra sells you as product-heavy, mid-to-senior talent. At an early startup that makes you a founding engineer. Founding engineers don't move tickets To-Do → Doing → Done — that's NPC behaviour, running a script on rails. You have agency.

Owns outcomes

Not tickets. Pushes the company forward, not just their own lane.

Knows the terrain

The industry, the major players, the market the startup is fighting in.

Takes initiative

Contributes product ideas. Doesn't wait to be told.

No handholding

Works harder than the average eng — and is trusted because of it.

03

Stay plugged in.

Cheap habits that signal founding-engineer behaviour. Subscribe to general tech newsletters — and crucially, your client's competitors' newsletters and LinkedIn.

> subscribe: ai.engineer/newsletter
> subscribe: <client's competitors>
> follow: competitors on LinkedIn / X
> action: SHARE findings + ideas in your updates

"Sharing an idea the founder already had still wins — it proves you're thinking about their business, not just your ticket."

05

Empathise with the founder.

The single most useful lens. The founder usually took a pay cut to do this. They're spending tens of thousands of dollars on you, and they answer to investors and deadlines.

It's their run. Play like a teammate who's there to win it — act how you'd want your engineer to act if it were your money and your dream.

06

Mind your stamina.

Hours are about output and reliability, not clocking time — but a 50-hour contract is a visible promise. From the client's POV you are always doing your contracted hours.

50hr contract
→
client expects MORE
→
same-or-less = worse than 40hr

Same start & end times

Consistency reads as reliability. Show up like clockwork.

Publish your hours

Tell the client when you're online and contactable. Slack notifications set for it — muted is fine.

07

Patience over panic.

More hours ≠ better engineer. 10x engineers don't work 400-hour weeks — they often work normal hours. Productivity is king: output per hour. Mashing the attack button — flailing more hours — gets you killed. Patience and timing clear the fight.

Use DND / Focus for deep-work blocks — most US clients are offline from ~11am our time (Xavier runs DND most of the day, checks in periodically). But DND ≠ MIA — stay reachable.

08

Under-promise. Over-deliver.

If you regularly work past your hours, the fix usually starts at estimation. Don't oversell — it only ends in disappointment. Beating your own deadline reads as far more valuable than hitting some "optimal" estimate.

honest_estimate = how long you really think
quote = honest_estimate * 1.5 # last 20% eats 80% of the time
# finishing early > hitting "optimal" > slipping a deadline

— Xavier: the last 20% takes 80% of the time, so add 50–60%. Worst case, you finish early. Clear the wall and you earn the leverage to lighten the load.

09

Leave a message.

Communication is seen more than code — don't measure yourself by lines pushed. In co-op, the players who call things out keep the whole party alive. Same here: good signals win.

"heads up — roadbump ahead, here's a new ETA"
"works on my machine ¯\_(ツ)_/¯"

"A quiet commit graph + loud communication beats loud commits + silence. Every time."

10

Party morale.

+10 MORALE

People keep around engineers they enjoy working with. Be that — and don't be intimidated into a grey background character.

Be a person

Weekend, hobbies, ask about theirs. Be yourself.

Be curious

About customers, product, parts of the business "above your pay grade."

Show up awake

They can tell when you slept at 4am and rolled into the meeting.

Keep your cool

Frustration is fine to name, not to vent. Cool off a few hours before reacting.

11

Rest at the campfire.

A consistent engineer beats a spiky one. Build a ritual that keeps you centred. (Xavier runs a daily checklist and a weekly checklist — it catches the things you'd almost skip.)

daily: standup ready · review inbox · re-read priorities · ship-or-update
weekly: demo progress · clean up · plan next · share a finding

The campfire isn't lost time. It's why you don't lose your run.

12
The village is under attack

Don't stand in the fire.

People don't handle fires well. Picture your house literally on fire. Are you still doing the dishes? No. You put out the fire — or you make damn sure it's being handled. Assume thousands of customers and tens of thousands in revenue are on the line.

Fire detected
→
Drop normal work (say so)
→
Put it out / confirm it's handled

Washing the dishes while the house burns is not "staying on task."

13

Into the fog.

You will get poorly-scoped work — it's the default when a company scales fast. Scoping it is your job. Alfie's checklist for any ambiguous or urgent task:

1

What & why

The context, the user pain, the product decisions behind it.

2

Impact

Does it solve the biggest pain? What % of users does it reach?

3

Urgency

When does the client actually expect it shipped?

4

Effort

How long does it really take to build?

5

Trade-offs

Deadline at risk? Fast vs right; cut to the must-haves.

6

Escalate

Realise you'll miss the agreed date? Flag it as early as possible, then re-run step 5.

Urgent + unclear? Don't Slack it to death — leave a clear message, then call them. — Falco

14

Try it solo first.

Earn the right to ask for help by showing you tried. Take a crack at it yourself before you call for backup. When you do ask, lead with what you tried.

1. what i tried: ...
2. what i think is happening: ...
3. the actual question: ...

Scout the area before you call for backup. Never make it look like you didn't put in the effort.

15
GAME OVER

…Continue? You respawn at the checkpoint. Every death teaches the level.

You're human. You will mess up. The differentiator is owning it instantly — people get genuinely frustrated when others don't fess up.

> "I messed up X."
> "Here's the impact + the fix."
> "Here's how it won't happen with Y."

And sometimes the client just isn't the right match for you. That's allowed.

16

Know your build.

You're an engineer, not a vibe coder. Understand what you built — know your build, not just which buttons you mashed. Sometimes you'll explain your approach to technical people. Be ready.

Understand it
→
Explain your approach
→
Easier collaboration + trust

Button-mashing clears the tutorial. It doesn't clear the boss.

17

Ways to get kicked.

A short, blunt list of things that actively damage you with clients.

"Works on my machine"

Clients hate this. Instant credibility hit. Don't.

Going MIA to "lock in"

Silence reads as a problem, not focus.

Blaming the client

Even when they're at fault, do it professionally or not at all. Defensive clients write you off for the rest of the contract.

Over-promising

Promise treasure, deliver a mimic. Don't sell what you can't actually deliver.

18

The little things.

Cheap, high-signal habits. Don't fumble the free points.

"be wary of tardiness"
✓ be 1 min early to every meeting
✓ same hours, every day
✓ share one finding / week
✓ own mistakes in one line

Tardiness really sets some people off, and it costs nothing to avoid.

19

Boss patterns.

Recognisable situations, with the move for each.

The task blew up past the deadline

Don't silently tank it — it makes great work look average and sets a brutal bar. Update ASAP with evidence + a new, generous ETA. Setback → "finishes ahead of schedule."

Blocked by someone else

Pass the blocked-potato — ping the blocker immediately and make sure your boss can see it's not you they're waiting on.

Client only talks when something breaks

Push for a sync; if hard, a daily digest or short Loom. Ask for feedback on good work too — it tells you where to keep pushing.

Unclear scope

Scope it yourself, get stakeholders on the hook, propose a call. (See: Into the fog.)
