# Points

## Founding engineers

- Lyra sells you as being product-heavy, mid to senior engineering talent.
- For early stage startups, generally this puts you in the category of “founding engineer”
- For more established startups, it’s less that, but still similar sort of thing.
- From the perspective of founders, founding engineers:
  - Are smart
  - Locked in
  - Know their shit
  - Gain an understanding of the startups position and broader market
  - Take initiative
  - Take ownership
  - Understand the business context
  - Push for the company to do better as a whole, not just in their own work

In other words

- Founding engineers don’t just move tickets from to do, to doing to done.
- Founding engineers should get an understanding of the industry, major players, subscribe to some newsletters
  - Competitors in particular are a good one to subscribe to
- They work harder than your average engineer
- They think about, contribute ideas to product
- They have a good product-sense
- Don’t need handholding

  - [https://www.ai.engineer/newsletter](https://www.ai.engineer/newsletter)
  - Any more that people recommend?
- **SHARE your findings and things you see from any of these, any ideas you get as a result \- customers love this. Even if they’ve thought of before it shows that you’re thinking deeply about your work and genuinely care**
- Subscribe to competitor newsletters, follow them on LikedIn etc

## Working hours

- You can be expected to work a bit of overtime (past your contracted hours of 40 or 50 hours) on given weeks \- but we don’t want you to be doing consistent overtime hours
  - This is a general expectation for professional work
- If you’re on a 50 hour contract then your client expects a greater output from you:
  - Quantity-wise
  - Quality-wise
  - If your output is the _same or less_ as a 40 hour work week engineer they have, this will make them even more upset:
    - “Especially considering they’re doing a 50 hour work week”
  - Always remember from the client POV you’re doing 50 hours per week if you’re on that contract

Hints/tips:

- Same start and end times.
- Let the client know when you’re actually working, contactable
  - Have Slack notifications setup for this \- you can have muted notifications around these all

## Work Intensity

- More hours doesn’t mean greater output or a greater engineer
  - 10x engineers don’t do 400 hours per week, often do normal hours
- Intensity and focus during work hours matters
  - PRODUCTIVITY IS KING
    - Output in an hour
- Contracts are calibrated so that you can work a standard 9-5 or 8-6
  - Intensity, focus can make you more productive

Hints/tips:

- If you’re getting spammed with Slack notifications/other notifications during work, struggling to do deep work:

    - Most clients are offline from our 11AM-ish, so from there its usually safer to turn on

    - VERY helpful for deep work

### Managing Your Work Hours

If you find yourself regularly working past your contracted hours:

- Set feasible expectations that are within your bandwidth. Don’t oversell yourself otherwise it’ll just lead to clients being disappointed.
  - A decent timeline proposal would be how long you think it’d actually take you and \+1 day to that timeline.
    - Xavier: last 20% takes 80% of the time, so IMO add 50-60%. Worse-case is you get it done faster
  - Seems counterintuitive because you’re actively proposing a slower timeline, but from experience finishing work before your proposed deadline shows more value than strictly following whatever objective timeline that seems “most optimal” for the average engineer.
- During trial often you’ll find yourself working way more than your contracted hours. This becomes the most difficult part of the contract because you’re actively setting expectations for your client whilst simultaneously trying to meet their expectations.
  - Ask lots of questions around the business context, product vision, add to ideas etc.
  - Being completely honest, most client trials will be incredibly difficult. This is essentially the client’s gamble on whether they’ll spend a chunk of their seeding round on you, so expect it to be hard.
  - However \- if you pass trial \- don’t expect the rest of the contract to have similar expectations.
  - If you’ve set a good impression on your trial (which you likely have if you passed) you can spend the following weeks applying time management strategies to lighten up/control your workload. Additionally, the client would also be more flexible to accommodate for any changes / feedback that would help your workflow.
  - Further on in the contract you have a lot more leverage because they’ve already invested a lot in you to build their product, and often it is much easier to collaborate changes to the work environment rather than suddenly drop you (especially in smaller contracts). Don’t be an ass about this tho, eventually they’ll find another engineer to replace you if they think you’re overall difficult to work with.
- Often communication is seen more than actual code.
  - Try not to base your output solely on how much code is pushed
  - Planning, scoping and discussing probably take more time than actually writing the code these days.
  - Don’t stress too much over not constantly pushing code if you’re already talking a lot with your client/s. Completely normal to put that in your updates

## General Things/No category

### Be compassionate to the founders

- They’re taking a pay cut to do this work usually
- They’re spending 10s of thousands of dollars on you
- You’re sold as a founding engineer

### You’re human

- You will make mistakes
- You should just immediately be clear, “hey I messed up with xxx \- I’ll make sure it doesn’t happen again with yyyy”
  - Very important \- it can be frustrating to some people if others don’t fess up to mistakes
- The client might not be the right match for you

### You’re an engineer not a vibe coder

- Understand your work
- Sometimes you might need to explain to technical people
- Regularly communicate how you approach problems and what you have considered, it shows you care & makes collaboration easier

### Dealing with ambiguity

- You will receive poorly scoped work
- Its your responsibility to scope and get stakeholders onboard
- Its your responsibility to explain to stakeholders why them not helping you makes it difficult to get the work done well
- From Alfie:
  - to add on this I think we should also touch on dealing with tight deadlines. very often these 2 comes together when a company is scaling fast.
  - imo we need to be able to understand:
  - 1\. what is that we're building and why we're building this (this involves understanding the context, understanding users pain points, discussing product decisions with the client etc)
  - 2\. how much impact does it have? (does it solve the most pain points for the users, what's the percentage of user this feature is aiming for etc)
  - 3\. how urgent it is (when does the client expect to have it shipped)
  - 4\. how long does it take to actually implement the feature
  - 5\. if you feel like you're not gonna make the ddl what are the tradeoffs we can make? (building it fast instead of building it right, cutdown the scope to keep only the must have and leave good to have for the future etc.)
  - 6\. if you half way through and realised you can't meet the original ddl you agreed with the client, escalate as early as possible and repeat the last step
- Dont rush in without understanding fully what needs to be done and what is expected. Dont rely on slack messages, if something is urgent and you need clarification, propose a sync with them directly so you can clear miscommunications via a call. (Especially when dealing with UI work, I (Falco) found this to be very helpful)

### Proactiveness

- When you’re stuck or need help on an issue that’s not small, always try your best to solve the problem first, and when you ask for help, mention what you’ve tried so far, your current understanding, and then ask the question. Don’t make it seem you haven’t put in the effort

# Presentation structure

Likely different clients may result in many different situations that can't be feasibly answered, let alone properly solved all in one 15-minute presentation. So the main focus of our presentation should be mindset \+ perspective focused to give engineers social tools to be able to handle these situations on their own. Towards the end of the lesson once ppl start conking out and losing attention we can actually attribute it to handling specific situations like expansive scope / communicating your contribution to the team.

Some general rules that blanket most situations

1. Under-promise and over-deliver
2. First impression is everything
3. Empathise with a founder \- responsible for multiple investors and deadlines \- gambling on a random Aussie engineer to carry their product. Put yourself in their shoes and act and communicate how you'd want your own engineer to communicate with you if you had those stakes yourself.

## Frequent situations

\> PM delegates a task to you and you both agree on a deadline. Eventually you discover that the problem is a lot more difficult than expected and would be incredibly taxing to get done by the deadline.

- Most of the time engineers would just tank this and get the work done (especially new engs when they first start on a client) because it's a clear way to gain props because you look _goated asf_ because you finished a difficult task quickly.
- There's many problems with this
  - Not properly communicating the difficulty / time it took just makes the client think that the work you did was _average_. This is EVEN WORSE if the client is non-technical.
  - Very easy way to burn yourself out in a week
  - You’ve now set this bar for yourself for all of your upcoming tasks.
- Easiest way to do this is to just update the client asap whenever you run into roadbumps, with explanations and evidence of what’s going on. Most importantly, on top of that quickly give them a new expected time to finish, ideally much further than you actually think you’ll be done \- you turn a setback of trust into an opportunity for you to make the client think you’re reliable to finish ahead of deadlines.

\> Unclear scope of a ticket, end up spending most of your time trying to figure out scope on your own / investigate it from other employees / client

\> Inadvertently blocked by someone you need context from to finish a ticket. Can also go for general situations when you’re blocked

- Pass the hot potato to them and make sure whoever your big boss is can see that clearly. The _blocked-potato_. Make people recognise asap that they’re not waiting on you. Ping that person immediately and make sure everyone knows that you did that.

\> You’ve got a client that only comms when there’s a bug or something wrong. You never really get feedback on good work, and you feel like you’re working blindly

- This sucks but there’s multiple things you can attempt
- Push for a proper sync meeting
- If the above is too difficult for the client a daily digest could be something the client could add in each day / provide a loom video discussing next steps
- Push for more feedback on positive changes \- can say the reason being it gives more direction when things are going well and can keep engs going in that direction.

## Some no-gos

- “It works on my machine, don’t see the issue” \-\> clients hate this shit
- Going MIA to “lock in”
- Directly blaming the client for issues. _Of course there’s many situations where the client is at fault but there’s professional and passive ways to communicate that you aren’t to blame. Some clients can get super defensive and can just think of you as someone impossible to work with for the rest of the contract_
